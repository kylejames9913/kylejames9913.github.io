VOL .01 : KELLY ISLAND

The Search for Fall Trout

Documented by Spencer Whitaker







With the arrival of frigid mornings and turning of leaves it is safe to say

that the summer season has ended. Fortunately fall is somewhat THE TIME to be on the river

as trout begin to gorge in preparation for winter. Big hoppers are still a mainstay as well

as the infamous October Caddis hatches and BWO's.

In an effort to take advantage of the daily feastings of these trout, CJ and I met in the morning

with high hopes of casting at rising cutthroat that would viciously inhale big hoppers.















Once we rigged up we began our search for cut banks and log jams that would typically hold risng fish.

We hiked in a bit and came across some water where we spotted trout working the surface, possibly tiny may flies.

Not interested in dinks we kept walking looking for better water.















We finally found some nice banks with deeper water and rising trout.

Immediately we began a delicate barage of hoppers in their feed lines with little interest.

We worked up the bank and log jams with no takers nor interest. Back to the drawing board.















After spending much time changing presentations and new water we made the call to go back and fish in wider water.

With the sun being on that section longer we hoped warmer water would encourage the fish to be more willing to eat.














Upon arrival conditions already were much better. Trout and bugs were working on the surface.

We changed flies accordingly only to be left confused as to why we had no risers or strikes.

As we laughed about our impotence as fly fisherman I tied back on a nymph which seemed to be

what the fish were keying on.

Within minutes I hooked into what we thought would be a decent rainbow only to net a Pike Minnow.

Negative on the fish count. Released with care.















Decided to call it a day and take our losses. Time on the water gives you experience

and I am afraid that is all we left with that day.

Out.
